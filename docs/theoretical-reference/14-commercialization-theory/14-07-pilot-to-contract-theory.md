# Pilot-To-Contract Theory

## 1. Purpose

This document defines the theory and structure for converting enterprise pilot engagements into full contracts. It establishes the principles, success criteria, evidence requirements, and conversion mechanisms that govern AQLIYA's pilot-to-contract process.

The purpose is not to create a sales playbook. It is to define the **structured proof-of-value doctrine** that ensures every pilot produces commercial evidence, every evidence chain converts to contractual terms, and every contract reflects the value that the platform has proven.

## 2. Thesis

**A pilot is a structured proof-of-value engagement that produces the evidence required for a contract decision. It is not a free trial, a feature evaluation, or a relationship-building exercise. It is a commercial event that produces commercial evidence. The conversion from pilot to contract follows the evidence: the pilot proves the value, the evidence justifies the terms, and the contract codifies the relationship.**

The pilot-to-contract process has five stages:

1. **Scoping:** Define the engagement boundaries, success criteria, and evidence collection plan before the pilot begins.
2. **Evidence Production:** Execute the pilot with structured evidence collection at every stage. The pilot produces the evidence that the contract will reference.
3. **Value Measurement:** Quantify the pilot outcomes using the Value Triad (time saved, risk reduced, trust earned) with level-specific, capability-attributed, engagement-evidenced metrics.
4. **Value Proof Presentation:** Present the pilot evidence as a commercial proof document that traces value claims to platform-produced evidence.
5. **Contract Structuring:** Convert the value proof into contractual terms — pricing, scope, governance, and expansion path — that reflect the evidenced value.

## 3. Problem

Pilot programs in enterprise software fail at conversion for predictable reasons:

- **Unstructured pilots** produce anecdotes, not evidence. "The team liked it" and "it saved some time" are sentiments, not commercial evidence.
- **Free pilots** attract tire-kickers who have no procurement authority or budget commitment. Free pilots have low conversion rates because the buyer has no investment in producing evidence.
- **Feature pilots** evaluate capabilities without measuring value. The buyer determines that the product "works" but cannot justify the investment because no value was measured.
- **Success criteria-free pilots** have no definition of success. The pilot ends, the buyer says "it was interesting," and there is no basis for a contract conversation.
- **Undefined pilots** expand scope continuously. New stakeholders, new requirements, and new use cases are added without re-scoping, extending the pilot indefinitely without progressing toward a contract.

AQLIYA faces an additional challenge: in regulated domains, buyers require evidence to justify technology investments. A pilot that does not produce governance-grade evidence cannot support a procurement decision in an audit firm or financial institution.

## 4. Why Existing Systems Fail

- **Free pilots** remove financial commitment from the buyer, which removes the incentive to define success criteria, allocate evaluation resources, and make procurement decisions. Free pilots convert at rates 40-60% lower than structured paid pilots in enterprise contexts.
- **Feature evaluation pilots** test whether the product works, not whether it produces value. In regulated domains, "it works" is necessary but not sufficient for procurement.
- **Time-limited trials** create artificial urgency without producing evidence. The buyer feels pressure to decide before the trial expires, but has no evidence to support the decision.
- **Generic success criteria** ("improve efficiency by 20%") are not measurable in a 60-90 day pilot. Domain-specific, platform-measurable criteria are required.
- **Post-pilot value gaps** occur when the pilot demonstrates the product but not the commercial value. The buyer is impressed but cannot justify the investment because value was not measured during the pilot.

## 5. AQLIYA Philosophy

1. **A pilot is a paid proof-of-value, not a free trial.** Financial commitment from the buyer ensures procurement seriousness and evidence production discipline.
2. **Every pilot has defined success criteria.** Without measurable success criteria, there is no basis for conversion. Success criteria are agreed before the pilot begins.
3. **The platform produces the evidence.** Value evidence is produced by AQLIYA during the pilot, not by manual analysis afterward. The product proves its own value.
4. **AI assists. Humans decide. Including the buyer.** AQLIYA provides the evidence. The buyer makes the procurement decision. No pressure, no manipulation, no artificial urgency.
5. **Governance in the pilot must be real.** A pilot with governance disabled or simplified produces no governance evidence. Governance must be active and realistic from day one.
6. **The pilot is the first real deployment.** Data, workflows, and governance configurations from the pilot carry forward to production. The pilot is not a throwaway environment.

## 6. Core Principles

1. **Paid, structured, time-bounded.** Every pilot has a price, a defined scope, measurable success criteria, and a fixed timeline. These four constraints produce commercial evidence.
2. **Success criteria are value criteria.** Pilot success is measured in value terms (time saved, risk reduced, trust earned), not feature terms (capabilities demonstrated, modules accessed).
3. **Evidence production is native, not manual.** The platform produces value evidence as a byproduct of normal use. No manual data collection, no external analysis, no post-hoc surveys.
4. **The pilot scope is the wedge scope.** Pilot scope is limited to the audit or financial review use case. Expansion to broader use cases is a contract conversation, not a pilot conversation.
5. **Pilot-to-contract is a conversion, not a restart.** The pilot environment, data, governance configurations, and evidence become the production deployment. No data migration, no configuration rebuild.
6. **Trust compounds across the pilot.** Each engagement cycle in the pilot produces more evidence, more trust, and clearer value proof. The pilot is not a single event — it is a compounding evidence sequence.

## 7. Key Concepts

- **Structured Proof-of-Value (SPOV):** The pilot methodology defined by AQLIYA: paid, scoped, evidenced, and conversion-oriented. Not a free trial or a feature evaluation.
- **Pilot Success Criteria (PSC):** The measurable, domain-specific, platform-producible value targets that define pilot success. Agreed before the pilot begins. Example: "14 PEH saved per engagement, 2 near-miss events identified, 95% governance compliance."
- **Evidence Collection Plan (ECP):** The pre-pilot document that specifies what evidence will be collected, how it will be measured, and how it will be presented. The ECP ensures that value measurement is built into the pilot, not added afterward.
- **Value Proof Document (VPD):** The post-pilot commercial document that presents pilot outcomes as value evidence. The VPD traces each value claim to platform-produced data and forms the basis for contract negotiation.
- **Pilot-to-Production Continuity:** The principle that the pilot environment and data carry forward to production. No data migration. No reconfiguration. The pilot is the first real deployment.
- **Conversion Gate:** The decision point where pilot evidence is evaluated against success criteria. If criteria are met, the pilot converts to contract. If not, the evidence is analyzed and the scope or criteria are revised.

## 8. Operational Implications

1. The commercial team must develop SPOV frameworks per domain. Audit SPOVs have different success criteria than financial review SPOVs.
2. Every pilot must have a signed SPOV agreement that specifies: scope, timeline, success criteria, pricing, and the pilot-to-production continuity commitment.
3. Customer success must be assigned to every pilot. They own evidence collection, value measurement, and the value proof presentation.
4. The pilot-to-contract timeline must be predictable. AQLIYA's target: 90-day pilot, 30-day contract negotiation, 14-day deployment. Total: ~5 months from engagement to production.
5. Failed pilots must be analyzed. Not every pilot converts. The evidence from failed pilots must inform product, sales, and scoping improvements.
6. The pilot price must be a meaningful commercial commitment — enough to filter out tire-kickers, not so much as to block serious evaluation.

## 9. Product Implications

1. The product must produce value evidence natively during pilots. Time metrics, risk events, governance compliance, and decision quality must be tracked without manual intervention.
2. Pilot environments must be production-equivalent. Feature limitations, data restrictions, or governance simplifications undermine evidence production.
3. The pilot-to-production transition must be seamless. Data, configurations, governance rules, and evidence history must carry forward without migration.
4. Value proof reports must be generable from within the platform. One-click export of pilot evidence in procurement-ready formats.
5. Success criteria dashboards must be visible to both AQLIYA and the buyer throughout the pilot. Both parties must see progress against the defined metrics.

## 10. Architecture Implications

1. Pilot environments must be architecturally identical to production environments. No feature flags, no data limitations, no performance degradation.
2. Evidence collection must be automatic. The platform must log time metrics, risk events, governance compliance, and decision quality without requiring user action.
3. The pilot-to-production transition must be a configuration change, not a data migration. Tenant data, governance rules, and evidence history must persist.
4. Success criteria must be configurable per pilot. Different buyers have different success metrics. The platform must support custom KPI tracking.
5. Self-hosted pilot environments must produce the same evidence as cloud environments. Pilot evidence must not be deployment-dependent.

## 11. Governance Implications

1. Governance must be fully active during the pilot. A pilot with simplified governance produces no governance evidence and undermines the trust model.
2. Governance compliance metrics must be included in pilot success criteria. "95% governance compliance rate" is as important as "14 PEH saved."
3. The governance configuration from the pilot must carry forward to production. The time invested in governance setup during the pilot is not lost.
4. Governance event logs from the pilot are key commercial evidence. Every approval, escalation, compliance check, and near-miss is a data point for the value proof.

## 12. AI / Intelligence Implications

1. The intelligence layer must be fully active during the pilot. Turning off AI during the pilot produces no intelligence evidence.
2. AI-attributed findings must be tracked and presented in the pilot value proof. "The intelligence layer identified X findings" is a measurable pilot outcome.
3. Intelligence model performance metrics must be included in pilot success criteria. Not ML metrics — domain metrics: finding accuracy, false positive rate, risk identification rate.
4. The intelligence model must improve during the pilot as it accumulates firm-specific data. This improvement is itself a value proof point: "the model became 12% more accurate over 4 engagement cycles."

## 13. UX Implications

1. Pilot success criteria must be visible in the product from day one. The buyer must see how progress maps to the agreed success metrics.
2. Evidence collection must be passive — the user should not need to select "collect evidence." It is automatic.
3. The value proof report must be one-click exportable. The buyer must be able to generate a procurement-ready document directly from the platform.
4. Pilot-to-contract transition UX must be clear. When the pilot concludes, the user must see a natural continuation — not a disruption.

## 14. Commercial Implications

1. Pilot pricing must reflect the value of the evidence produced. A paid pilot that produces commercial evidence is worth more to both parties than a free pilot that produces anecdotes.
2. Pilot success criteria must be agreed before the pilot begins. This ensures both parties have a shared definition of success and a basis for contract negotiation.
3. The contract must be structured based on pilot evidence. Pricing, scope, and terms reflect the value proven during the pilot — not the value projected during the sales process.
4. The pilot-to-contract conversion rate must be tracked as a key commercial metric. A high conversion rate validates the SPOV methodology. A low conversion rate requires scoping, criteria, or product adjustments.
5. Failed pilots are not wasted. Pilot evidence — even from failed pilots — improves scoping, product, and sales for future opportunities.

## 15. Anti-Patterns

1. **Free Pilot Without Structure.** Offering a free pilot to "let the team try it." No success criteria, no evidence collection, no commercial framework. Low conversion, high resource cost, no evidence.
2. **Feature Evaluation Pilot.** Defining pilot success as "the product works" or "the team liked the features." This produces no value evidence and no basis for infrastructure pricing.
3. **Unlimited Pilot Scope.** Allowing the buyer to expand the pilot scope indefinitely. Each scope expansion delays conversion and dilutes evidence. Scope must be fixed before the pilot begins.
4. **Pilot-Production Gap.** Running the pilot in a simplified environment that cannot convert to production. This forces a restart and loses the evidence continuity.
5. **Post-Pilot Value Gap.** Completing the pilot without producing a value proof document. Without a formal VPD, the pilot evidence dissipates into anecdotes.
6. **Governance-Lite Pilot.** Running the pilot with simplified or disabled governance. No governance evidence means no trust-based selling and no infrastructure pricing justification.
7. **Single-Cycle Pilot.** Ending the pilot after one engagement cycle. The compounding effect requires at least 2-3 cycles to become visible. Single-cycle pilots understate the value.

## 16. Examples

**Example 1: Audit Firm SPOV.** A mid-tier audit firm (45 partners, 320 engagements/year) enters a 90-day SPOV:
- Scope: 3 real audit engagements using AuditOS with full governance and intelligence.
- Success criteria: (a) 12+ PEH saved per engagement, (b) 2+ near-miss events identified per engagement, (c) 95%+ governance compliance rate.
- Pilot price: $45,000 (creditable toward annual contract).
- Evidence collection: Automatic platform tracking of time, risk, and governance metrics.
- Outcome: After 3 engagements, the pilot produces evidence of 14.2 PEH saved, 3.1 near-miss events per engagement, 97.3% governance compliance. The VPD is presented, and the contract is structured based on the evidenced value.

**Example 2: Financial Review SPOV Expansion.** An audit firm that has been using AuditOS for 12 months is presented with a Financial Intelligence SPOV:
- Scope: 4 financial review cycles for the firm's advisory practice.
- Success criteria: (a) 10+ PEH saved per review cycle, (b) 1+ material risk identified, (c) evidence completeness rate 90%+.
- The SPOV leverages existing trust and evidence from the audit deployment. Conversion is faster because the trust compounding has already begun.

**Example 3: Failed Pilot Analysis.** A Big 4 engagement team pilot does not meet success criteria. Analysis reveals: (a) governance configuration was too complex for the pilot timeline, (b) success criteria were set too aggressively for a 3-engagement pilot, (c) the firm's engagement structure required customization that exceeded pilot scope. The scoping framework is updated, and the firm is offered a revised pilot with adjusted scope and criteria.

## 17. Enterprise Impact

1. **Higher conversion rates.** Structured, paid, evidentiary pilots convert at 2-3x the rate of free, unstructured pilots.
2. **Faster contract cycles.** Defined success criteria and evidence production eliminate the "evaluation limbo" that extends pilot timelines.
3. **Higher contract values.** Contracts based on evidenced value command infrastructure pricing. Contracts based on feature evaluation command tool pricing.
4. **Lower implementation risk.** Pilot-to-production continuity eliminates the implementation gap that plagues traditional enterprise sales.
5. **Compounding commercial advantage.** Each successful pilot produces evidence that accelerates future pilots. The SPOV library grows with each conversion.

## 18. Long-Term Strategic Importance

The Pilot-To-Contract Theory is the commercial engine of AQLIYA's go-to-market. Every account enters through a pilot. Every contract is justified by pilot evidence. Every expansion begins with a mini-SPOV in the new domain.

Over time, the SPOV methodology becomes a competitive advantage. It produces better evidence, faster conversions, and higher contract values than any alternative approach. It also serves as a qualification mechanism: buyers who will not commit to a structured pilot are unlikely to become long-term infrastructure customers.

The strategic alignment between product and commercial process is critical. A product built on evidence, governance, and human-in-the-loop decision intelligence must be sold through an evidence-based, governed, human-decision process. The Pilot-To-Contract Theory ensures this alignment.
