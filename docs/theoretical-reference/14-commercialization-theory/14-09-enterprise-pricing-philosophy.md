# Enterprise Pricing Philosophy

## 1. Purpose

This document defines AQLIYA's pricing philosophy for Enterprise Decision Intelligence infrastructure. It establishes the principles, structures, and doctrine that govern how AQLIYA prices its platform, its domain wedges, its deployment models, and its expansion paths — in a way that is coherent with the category thesis and the commercial model.

The purpose is not to define specific price points. It is to define the **pricing doctrine** that ensures every pricing decision reinforces AQLIYA's positioning as decision infrastructure, not a tool, not a feature, not a generic SaaS product.

## 2. Thesis

**AQLIYA is priced as infrastructure, not as a tool. Infrastructure pricing captures the structural value that decision intelligence creates for regulated enterprises: time saved, risk reduced, trust earned. It does not price per seat, per feature, or per usage — because those models price tools, not infrastructure. Infrastructure pricing is value-based, domain-depth-weighted, and expansion-oriented. It reflects what AQLIYA is, not what it costs to deliver.**

The pricing philosophy has five interlocking principles:

1. **Value-based, not cost-based.** AQLIYA is priced on the value it creates (time, risk, trust) — not on the cost of delivering the software. Cost-plus pricing produces tool pricing; value-based pricing produces infrastructure pricing.
2. **Domain-depth-weighted.** Pricing reflects the depth of domain intelligence applied. Audit pricing reflects audit domain depth. Financial intelligence pricing reflects financial domain depth. Deeper domain intelligence commands higher pricing.
3. **Expansion-oriented.** The initial sale (AuditOS) is the entry point, not the ceiling. Pricing is structured so that expansion revenue (financial intelligence, governance operations) compounds over time.
4. **Deployment-agnostic with sovereignty premium.** The same decision intelligence infrastructure is priced at parity across cloud and self-hosted. Self-hosted and air-gapped deployments command a sovereignty premium, not a discount.
5. **No category-damaging discounts.** Discounts that position AQLIYA as a tool rather than infrastructure are rejected. The price reflects the category, not competitive pressure.

## 3. Problem

Enterprise software pricing is dominated by models that do not fit AQLIYA's category:

- **Per-seat pricing** reduces infrastructure to a personnel cost. It incentivizes seat count growth, not value capture. It makes AQLIYA comparable to audit tools and compliance platforms that are priced per user.
- **Per-feature pricing** reduces infrastructure to a feature list. It incentivizes feature accumulation, not depth. It makes AQLIYA comparable to SaaS products that compete on feature breadth.
- **Usage-based pricing** reduces infrastructure to a consumption metric. It disincentivizes adoption (use more, pay more). It makes AQLIYA comparable to API services that charge per call.
- **Freemium models** reduce infrastructure to a sampling mechanism. They attract users who are not enterprise buyers and devalue the category by making core capabilities available for free.

Each of these models prices the wrong variable. AQLIYA creates structural value through governed, evidence-backed, intelligence-augmented decision workflows. The pricing must capture this structural value — not the number of users, features, or API calls.

## 4. Why Existing Systems Fail

- **Per-seat SaaS pricing** was designed for collaboration tools where each user creates incremental value. In decision intelligence, the value is in the platform's structural capability — not in the number of users who access it. Five partners using AuditOS with full governance and intelligence produce more value than 50 staff members using it for evidence collection.
- **Feature-based tiering** (Basic, Pro, Enterprise) conflates depth with breadth. AQLIYA's value is in domain depth, not feature breadth. A "Basic" tier with governance disabled is not AQLIYA — it is a different product.
- **Discount-based enterprise sales** erode infrastructure positioning. A 50% discount to close a deal signals that the published price is negotiable and the real value is lower than claimed.
- **Consumption pricing** penalizes adoption. In decision intelligence, the goal is more usage, more engagement cycles, more evidence accumulation. Charging per transaction disincentivizes the behavior that produces the most value.
- **Competitive price-matching** positions AQLIYA as comparable to audit tools. If AQLIYA matches audit tool pricing, it is implicitly categorized as an audit tool — not decision intelligence infrastructure.

## 5. AQLIYA Philosophy

1. **Price follows category.** Decision intelligence infrastructure is priced as infrastructure. Audit tools are priced as tools. AQLIYA's price must reflect what it is, not what competitors charge.
2. **Value is the pricing unit.** The pricing variable is the value AQLIYA creates: time saved, risk reduced, trust earned. These are the units that justify infrastructure pricing.
3. **Expansion is the revenue model.** The initial sale is the wedge entry. Revenue compounds through expansion from audit to financial intelligence to governance operations. Pricing must facilitate — not obstruct — this expansion.
4. **Governance is priced in, not on.** Governance is not a premium add-on. It is structural to the platform. Pricing governance separately would signal that governance is optional — which contradicts AQLIYA's category thesis.
5. **Self-hosted is not a discount.** Self-hosted and air-gapped deployments deliver additional value (sovereignty, control, continuity). They are priced at a premium, not a discount.
6. **No discount that damages the category.** A discount that positions AQLIYA as a tool (per-seat, per-feature, freemium) is a category violation. Short-term revenue does not justify long-term category damage.

## 6. Core Principles

1. **Infrastructure pricing, not tool pricing.** AQLIYA is priced on the structural value it creates for the enterprise, not on the number of users, features, or transactions.
2. **Value-based tiers, not feature-based tiers.** Tiers reflect domain depth and value creation, not feature availability. Every tier includes the core platform (governance, evidence, intelligence). Tiers differ in domain application and value scope.
3. **Wedge-entry pricing facilitates expansion.** The initial audit engagement is priced to enter the account and prove value. It is not the revenue ceiling — it is the floor.
4. **Expansion pricing compounds.** Each domain expansion (audit → financial intelligence → governance) adds incremental value and incremental pricing. Expansion revenue grows faster than initial revenue.
5. **Professional services are a value stream.** Implementation, governance configuration, and operational support are priced as value-adding services, not as cost recovery.
6. **Pricing transparency reinforces trust.** Pricing must be transparent, consistent, and defensible. Opaque pricing and inconsistent discounts undermine the trust model that the product depends on.

## 7. Key Concepts

- **Infrastructure Pricing Model:** Pricing based on the structural value the platform creates for the enterprise (time, risk, trust) rather than on delivery costs (seats, features, usage).
- **Domain Depth Pricing:** Pricing that reflects the depth of domain intelligence applied. Deeper domain intelligence (more specialized models, more domain-specific workflows, more governance rules) commands higher pricing.
- **Wedge Entry Pricing:** The initial pricing for AuditOS — lower than full platform pricing but sufficient to establish infrastructure positioning and prove value within the account.
- **Expansion Multiplier:** The pricing increase when expanding from one domain wedge to multiple. Revenue per account compounds as domains expand from audit → financial intelligence → governance.
- **Sovereignty Premium (SP):** The premium applied to self-hosted and air-gapped deployments reflecting the additional value of data sovereignty, operational control, and continuity guarantees.
- **Value Tier Structure:** Pricing tiers based on value scope (domain application, enterprise breadth, governance depth) rather than feature availability. Every tier includes the full platform; tiers differ in the breadth and depth of domain application.

## 8. Operational Implications

1. Sales must present pricing in value terms, not cost terms. "This investment reflects the decision intelligence value for your audit practice" — not "this is the cost per seat."
2. Pricing must be consistent across accounts. Segment-specific discounts that undermine infrastructure positioning are not permitted. Volume discounts must be structured as multi-domain expansions, not per-seat discounts.
3. Customer success must track value realization against pricing. If an account is not realizing value proportional to its pricing, the issue is value realization, not pricing adjustment.
4. Professional services pricing must be distinct from platform pricing. Services are a value stream, not a subsidy. Implementation, governance configuration, and training are priced at market rates.
5. Finance must model revenue as wedge entry + expansion + services. The initial contract is the floor. Revenue compounds through domain expansion and value realization.
6. Competitive pricing intelligence must inform positioning, not pricing decisions. AQLIYA prices based on value, not competition. Competitive data informs how to present value, not how to set prices.

## 9. Product Implications

1. The product must expose value metrics that justify pricing. Time saved, risk reduced, governance compliance, evidence completeness — these are the data that defend infrastructure pricing.
2. Platform capabilities (governance, evidence, intelligence, workflow) must not be tiered by availability. Every pricing tier includes the full platform. Tiers differ in domain application, not feature access.
3. The product must support multi-domain expansion within a single account. Audit, financial intelligence, and governance workflows must be additive, not separate deployments.
4. Self-hosted and cloud deployments must deliver the same product experience. Pricing differs based on value (sovereignty premium), not product capability.
5. Value dashboards must show pricing-justifying metrics. The user must see the value data that supports their investment level.

## 10. Architecture Implications

1. Multi-tenant and self-hosted architectures must support the same pricing model. Pricing is not determined by deployment model.
2. Domain-specific workflow engines (audit, financial intelligence, governance) must be independently licensable and additively composable within the same platform instance.
3. Usage metering must track value metrics, not cost metrics. Time-to-decision, evidence completeness, risk events, governance compliance — not API calls or data volume.
4. Enterprise-level billing must support multi-domain contracts where audit, financial intelligence, and governance are priced and billed as additive value layers.
5. The platform must support pricing-configureable governance depth. Advanced governance configuration and custom governance rules may justify higher pricing tiers.

## 11. Governance Implications

1. Governance is never a premium add-on. Governance is a core platform capability that is included in every pricing tier. Separating governance from the base platform contradicts AQLIYA's category thesis.
2. Governance depth may vary by pricing tier. Deeper governance configuration, more custom rules, and more governance analytics may be available at higher tiers — but basic governance is universal.
3. The pricing model must reflect that governance is structural, not procedural. Structural governance (embedded in the platform, always active) is what justifies infrastructure pricing.

## 12. AI / Intelligence Implications

1. Intelligence augmentation is a core platform capability, not a premium add-on. Every pricing tier includes intelligence augmentation. Separating AI from the base platform contradicts the category thesis.
2. Domain-specific intelligence models (audit, financial, governance) may be priced additively. Expanding from audit intelligence to financial intelligence adds domain depth and value.
3. Intelligence value must be evidenced in pricing conversations. "The intelligence layer identified X additional findings worth Y in risk avoidance" justifies pricing better than "we use advanced AI."

## 13. UX Implications

1. The UX must make value visible in pricing-justifying terms. Value dashboards show the metrics that support the enterprise's investment.
2. Multi-domain expansion must be seamless in the product. Adding financial intelligence or governance workflows to an existing audit deployment should be a configuration, not a migration.
3. Pricing-tier differentiation must not be visible as feature restrictions. All tiers deliver the full platform. Tiers differ in scope and depth, not capability.

## 14. Commercial Implications

1. Contracts are multi-year with embedded value proof. Renewal is justified by documented value, not vendor relationships.
2. Initial contracts are structured as wedge entry with a defined expansion path. The initial pricing is the floor; expansion pricing compounds.
3. Professional services are priced and billed separately. Implementation, governance configuration, and training are billable services that add value and reinforce the infrastructure position.
4. Self-hosted and air-gapped deployments command a sovereignty premium. They are never priced below cloud.
5. Pricing consistency across accounts is essential. Account-specific discounts that undermine infrastructure pricing are not permitted. Volume is rewarded through multi-year, multi-domain contracts — not per-seat discounts.
6. The pricing model must facilitate expansion. The commercial structure must make it economically rational for the buyer to expand from audit to financial intelligence to governance within AQLIYA rather than adding a separate tool.

## 15. Anti-Patterns

1. **Per-Seat Pricing.** Pricing based on the number of users. This positions AQLIYA as a personnel tool, not infrastructure. It also creates a ceiling on revenue that does not reflect the value the platform creates.
2. **Feature-Based Tiering.** Pricing tiers based on feature availability. A "Basic" tier without governance is not AQLIYA. Feature restrictions contradict the category and reduce the value proof potential.
3. **Discount-Led Closing.** Using discounts to close deals. Discounts erode infrastructure positioning, create pricing inconsistency, and train buyers to negotiate on cost rather than value.
4. **Usage-Based Pricing.** Charging per transaction, per API call, or per data volume. This disincentivizes adoption and makes AQLIYA comparable to API services.
5. **Freemium or Free Tier.** Offering core capabilities for free. This devalues the category and attracts non-enterprise users who are not AQLIYA's target market.
6. **Governance-as-Add-On.** Pricing governance as a separate premium module. Governance is structural, not optional. Pricing it separately signals that governance can be removed — which contradicts AQLIYA's thesis.
7. **Self-Hosted Discount.** Pricing self-hosted deployment below cloud pricing. Self-hosted delivers additional value and commands a premium, not a discount.
8. **Competitive Price-Matching.** Matching the pricing of audit tools or compliance platforms. AQLIYA is not a tool. It is infrastructure. Price-matching a tool positions AQLIYA as a tool.

## 16. Examples

**Example 1: Wedge Entry Pricing for Audit Firm.** A mid-tier audit firm (45 partners, 320 engagements/year) contracts AuditOS:
- Platform fee: Based on audit practice value (PEH saved, risk avoided, governance compliance). Covers the full platform: governance, evidence, intelligence, workflow.
- Domain fee: Audit domain intelligence, audit-specific workflows, audit governance rules. Included in initial contract as the wedge entry.
- Professional services: Implementation, governance configuration, training. Billed separately.
- Expansion path: Financial Intelligence domain layer available as expansion. Priced additively based on financial review value.
- Self-hosted option: 15-20% sovereignty premium for self-hosted deployment.

**Example 2: Expansion Pricing.** An audit firm using AuditOS for 12 months with evidenced value of 14.2 PEH per engagement, 31% more material findings, and 3 near-miss events per engagement:
- AuditOS contract renewal at current rate (value justified by evidenced outcomes).
- Financial Intelligence expansion layer: Additive pricing based on the projected value of applying the same decision intelligence infrastructure to financial review workflows. The expansion leverages existing evidence, governance, and trust.
- Total account value compounds: Year 1 = audit wedge. Year 2 = audit + financial intelligence. Year 3 = audit + financial intelligence + governance operations. Revenue growth is expansion-driven, not seat-driven.

**Example 3: What Pricing Does Not Look Like.** AQLIYA does NOT price as:
- "$150/user/month for Basic, $300/user/month for Pro, $500/user/month for Enterprise." This is tool pricing.
- "Free for up to 5 users, then $200/user/month." This is freemium pricing.
- "$0.10 per API call." This is consumption pricing.
- "$50K/year, 30% discount for multi-year." This is discount-led pricing.
- "Same price as [competitor audit tool]." This is competitive price-matching.

## 17. Enterprise Impact

1. **Higher revenue per account.** Infrastructure pricing captures more value than tool pricing. Multi-domain expansion compounds revenue within each account.
2. **Predictable expansion revenue.** Defined expansion paths (audit → financial intelligence → governance) produce predictable revenue growth.
3. **Pricing consistency.** Standardized pricing based on value, not negotiation, reduces deal-to-deal variance and reinforces the category.
4. **Stronger competitive positioning.** Pricing that reflects infrastructure value positions AQLIYA in a different category from audit tools. The comparison is not feature-to-feature but category-to-category.
5. **Higher retention.** Accounts that realize value proportional to their investment have stronger renewal justification. Infrastructure with embedded governance and evidence history is harder to replace than a tool.

## 18. Long-Term Strategic Importance

Pricing is one of the most powerful category signals. AQLIYA priced like an audit tool will be perceived as an audit tool. AQLIYA priced like decision intelligence infrastructure will be perceived as decision intelligence infrastructure.

The long-term risk of misaligned pricing is category drift. Per-seat pricing, feature-based tiering, and discount-led closing gradually reposition AQLIYA from infrastructure to tool. The market categorizes products by how they are priced. If AQLIYA is priced like_audit software, it will be compared to audit software, evaluated against audit software, and replaced by audit software.

The long-term opportunity of aligned pricing is category ownership. Infrastructure pricing, value-based tiers, and expansion-oriented contracts establish AQLIYA in its own category. Buyers who understand value-based pricing are the right buyers. Buyers who insist on per-seat pricing are the wrong buyers for AQLIYA's category.
