# Self-Hosted Value Theory

## 1. Purpose

This document defines the value theory for AQLIYA's self-hosted and air-gapped deployment models. It establishes why self-hosted deployment is a value multiplier — not a value compromise or a pricing discount — and how the commercial model captures the additional value that self-hosting creates for regulated enterprise buyers.

The purpose is not to define deployment architecture. It is to establish the **commercial doctrine** that governs how AQLIYA prices, sells, and supports self-hosted deployments in a way that reinforces the category thesis rather than undermining it.

## 2. Thesis

**Self-hosted deployment is not a cost concession — it is a value premium. Regulated enterprises that require data sovereignty, air-gapped operations, or infrastructure control are not choosing self-hosting because it is cheaper. They are choosing it because their regulatory, security, or operational requirements demand it. AQLIYA's self-hosted deployment delivers the same decision intelligence infrastructure — including governance, evidence, and intelligence augmentation — with additional value derived from data sovereignty and infrastructure control.**

Self-hosted value has three dimensions:

1. **Sovereignty Value:** The commercial value of maintaining data within the enterprise's own infrastructure, under its own governance, subject to its own regulatory jurisdiction. For regulated buyers, this is not optional — it is a prerequisite.
2. **Control Value:** The commercial value of controlling deployment, updates, integration, and operational timing. Enterprises with complex IT environments or strict change management processes value this control.
3. **Continuity Value:** The commercial value of operational continuity. Air-gapped deployments guarantee availability regardless of cloud provider outages, connectivity issues, or geopolitical disruptions.

## 3. Problem

Self-hosted deployment is often treated as a pricing problem — a cost that must be subsidized or a feature that must be discounted. This treatment is fundamentally wrong for AQLIYA's market:

- **Regulated enterprises do not choose self-hosting to save money.** They choose it because their regulatory framework, data classification policy, or security posture requires it. Offering a discount for self-hosting signals that it is less valuable — which is the opposite of the truth.
- **Self-hosted deployments require more, not less, of AQLIYA's core capabilities.** The same governance engine, evidence lifecycle, intelligence layer, and decision workflow must operate without cloud dependencies. This is a more demanding — not a less demanding — deployment.
- **Self-hosted buyers have higher trust requirements.** They require additional assurance that governance is active, evidence is complete, and intelligence is auditable — all within their own infrastructure. Meeting these requirements produces more trust, not less.

## 4. Why Existing Systems Fail

- **Cloud-only SaaS** cannot serve regulated enterprises that require data sovereignty. These buyers are excluded entirely, not converted at a discount.
- **Discounted self-hosted tiers** signal that self-hosting is less valuable. They attract cost-sensitive buyers rather than sovereignty-motivated buyers. They also undercut the commercial model by pricing infrastructure value at tool cost.
- **Feature-limited self-hosted** removes governance, intelligence, or evidence capabilities from self-hosted deployments. This produces a product that cannot deliver AQLIYA's category promise within the buyer's own infrastructure.
- **Standalone on-premise software** lacks the infrastructure capabilities (API integrations, workflow engine, governance automation) that make AQLIYA valuable. It is a regression to installed software, not a deployment model for decision intelligence.
- **Hybrid commercial models** that charge less for self-hosted and more for cloud create perverse incentives. Regulated enterprises choose self-hosted for compliance, not to save money. Pricing self-hosted lower trains buyers to devalue it.

## 5. AQLIYA Philosophy

1. **Self-hosted is a value premium, not a cost concession.** Self-hosted deployments deliver the same decision intelligence infrastructure with additional sovereignty, control, and continuity value. They are priced as a premium.
2. **Same product, different deployment.** AQLIYA's governance engine, evidence lifecycle, intelligence layer, and decision workflows are identical across cloud, private cloud, self-hosted, and air-gapped deployments. The product is not degraded for self-hosting.
3. **Data sovereignty is a commercial asset.** The ability to operate within the buyer's infrastructure, subject to their jurisdiction, under their governance — without cloud dependency — is a feature that commands premium pricing in regulated markets.
4. **Governance is deployment-agnostic.** Governance rules, evidence chains, and audit logs must be identical across all deployment models. Governance is not a cloud-only feature.
5. **AI assists in every deployment model.** The intelligence layer must operate in self-hosted and air-gapped environments. Intelligence augmentation is not a cloud dependency.

## 6. Core Principles

1. **Feature parity across deployments.** Same governance engine, same evidence lifecycle, same intelligence layer, same decision workflows. Deployment model does not determine capability.
2. **Pricing reflects value, not cost structure.** Self-hosted pricing reflects the value of sovereignty, control, and continuity — not the cost of AQLIYA's infrastructure. Self-hosted is priced at parity or premium, not at a discount.
3. **Governance is universal.** Every deployment model implements the same governance model. A self-hosted deployment has the same governance capabilities as a cloud deployment.
4. **Evidence chains are deployment-portable.** Evidence, decisions, and governance logs are owned by the enterprise, not locked into a deployment model. Enterprises can migrate between deployment models without losing evidence.
5. **Operational support scales with value.** Self-hosted deployments receive dedicated operational support because the operational requirements are higher, not lower. Support is a value driver, not a cost center.

## 7. Key Concepts

- **Sovereignty Value Multiplier (SVM):** The additional value that enterprises derive from maintaining data within their own infrastructure. SVM is highest for firms subject to data localization regulations, client data restrictions, or government security requirements.
- **Control Value Multiplier (CVM):** The additional value that enterprises derive from controlling deployment timing, update cycles, and infrastructure integration. CVM is highest for enterprises with complex IT environments, strict change management, or regulatory-mandated update cycles.
- **Continuity Value Multiplier (CoVM):** The additional value that enterprises derive from operational continuity guarantees. CoVM is highest for enterprises that cannot tolerate cloud dependency risks — outage, connectivity, or geopolitical.
- **Deployment Parity Principle:** All AQLIYA capabilities are available in all deployment models. The product is not degraded for self-hosting.
- **Self-Hosted Premium (SHP):** The pricing premium applied to self-hosted deployments reflecting sovereignty, control, and continuity value. SHP is not a discount — it is a premium.
- **Air-Gapped Operations Capability (AGOC):** The ability to operate AQLIYA's full decision intelligence infrastructure in a completely disconnected environment. AGOC is the highest-value deployment model for the most regulated buyers.

## 8. Operational Implications

1. Sales must present self-hosted deployment as a value premium, not a cost option. "Self-hosted deployment provides data sovereignty, operational control, and continuity guarantees that cloud deployment cannot. The premium reflects this additional value."
2. Customer success must provide dedicated support for self-hosted deployments. Operational requirements are higher, and support must match.
3. Marketing must position self-hosted deployment as a capability, not a concession. Case studies must feature regulated enterprises that chose self-hosted for sovereignty reasons.
4. Professional services for self-hosted deployment must be a revenue stream, not a cost center. Implementation, configuration, and governance setup for self-hosted environments require specialized expertise.
5. The product team must maintain feature parity across deployment models. Any capability available in cloud must be available in self-hosted and air-gapped.

## 9. Product Implications

1. Feature parity is non-negotiable. Governance, evidence, intelligence, and workflow capabilities must be identical in cloud, private cloud, self-hosted, and air-gapped deployments.
2. The intelligence layer must support on-premise and air-gapped operation. Intelligence augmentation is not a cloud-only feature. Model deployment must be compatible with disconnected environments.
3. Self-hosted deployment must include the same evidence lifecycle, governance automation, and decision workflow capabilities as cloud deployment. No degradation.
4. Migration between deployment models must be seamless. An enterprise that starts on cloud must be able to migrate to self-hosted without losing data, governance configuration, or evidence history.
5. Self-hosted deployments must produce the same value metrics as cloud deployments. Time savings, risk reduction, and governance compliance must be measurable identically across deployments.

## 10. Architecture Implications

1. The platform must support four deployment models: cloud, private cloud, self-hosted, and air-gapped. All must run the same core services.
2. The intelligence layer must support offline model operation. Model weights, inference, and domain models must be deployable without cloud connectivity.
3. Governance configuration, evidence storage, and audit logs must be locally operable in self-hosted environments. No cloud dependency for core data.
4. Update delivery for self-hosted environments must be through controlled release channels, not automatic cloud updates. Enterprises must control update timing.
5. Self-hosted infrastructure must support the same API integrations, workflow automation, and third-party connections as cloud — operating through enterprise-managed integration points.

## 11. Governance Implications

1. Governance rules must be identical across deployment models. A governance check that runs in cloud must produce the same result in self-hosted.
2. Governance logs in self-hosted environments must be locally stored, locally accessible, and locally auditable. Cloud access to governance logs is not required.
3. Self-hosted governance must support enterprise-specific compliance requirements that go beyond AQLIYA defaults. Configurable governance rules that operate locally.
4. Air-gapped environments must implement the same governance model without any external dependencies. Governance is structural, not cloud-dependent.

## 12. AI / Intelligence Implications

1. The intelligence layer must operate in all deployment models. On-premise model deployment, air-gapped model operation, and local inference are requirements — not optional features.
2. Intelligence performance in self-hosted environments must be comparable to cloud. Enterprises that choose self-hosted for compliance must not sacrifice intelligence capability.
3. Model updates for self-hosted environments must be delivered through controlled, enterprise-approved release packages — not automatic cloud updates.
4. Intelligence attribution must function identically in self-hosted and cloud environments. The same evidence of AI contribution, the same audit trails.

## 13. UX Implications

1. The UX must be identical across deployment models. Users should not be able to tell which deployment model they are using based on the interface.
2. Value metrics and dashboards must display the same data in self-hosted and cloud environments. Self-hosted users must not receive a degraded reporting experience.
3. Configuration and administration surfaces must support self-hosted operations: local update management, local governance configuration, local audit log access.
4. Air-gapped environments must have complete self-service support for configuration and governance setup. No cloud dependency for day-to-day operations.

## 14. Commercial Implications

1. Self-hosted pricing is at parity or premium to cloud pricing. Self-hosting is not a discount tier — it is a value tier.
2. Professional services for self-hosted deployment are a revenue stream. Implementation, configuration, governance setup, and operational support are billable services.
3. Self-hosted contracts include operational support SLAs that reflect the higher operational requirements. Support is premium, not basic.
4. The sovereignty, control, and continuity value must be explicitly quantified in commercial proposals. "Self-hosted deployment provides X in sovereignty value, Y in control value, and Z in continuity value."
5. Air-gapped deployment commands the highest premium. It serves the most regulated, most security-conscious, most sovereignty-motivated buyers in the market.
6. Expansion from cloud to self-hosted is an upgrade, not a migration. An enterprise that moves from cloud to self-hosted is increasing value, not changing cost.

## 15. Anti-Patterns

1. **Self-Hosted as Discount Tier.** Offering self-hosted deployment at a lower price than cloud. This signals that self-hosted is less valuable and attracts cost-motivated rather than sovereignty-motivated buyers.
2. **Feature-Limited Self-Hosted.** Removing governance, intelligence, or evidence capabilities from self-hosted deployments. This produces a product that cannot deliver AQLIYA's category promise.
3. **Cloud-Only Intelligence.** Making the intelligence layer dependent on cloud connectivity. This creates a degraded product for self-hosted users and a cloud dependency that contradicts sovereignty value.
4. **Self-Hosted as Afterthought.** Designing cloud-first and porting to self-hosted as a secondary deployment. Self-hosted must be a first-class deployment model, architecturally.
5. **Governance Degradation in Self-Hosted.** Simplifying or removing governance capabilities in self-hosted deployments. Governance is structural and must be deployment-agnostic.
6. **Cost-Based Self-Hosted Pricing.** Pricing self-hosted based on AQLIYA's infrastructure cost savings rather than the buyer's sovereignty, control, and continuity value.

## 16. Examples

**Example 1: Sovereignty Value for a Government Audit Office.** A national audit office requires all audit data to remain within national borders, subject to national data sovereignty regulations. Cloud deployment is not compliant. Self-hosted deployment with full governance, evidence, and intelligence — on national infrastructure — is not a cost savings. It is a compliance requirement that commands a 20% premium over cloud pricing because it delivers sovereignty value that cloud cannot.

**Example 2: Air-Gapped Value for a Defense Financial Review Unit.** A defense ministry financial review team operates in a completely disconnected environment. Cloud connectivity is prohibited. AQLIYA's air-gapped deployment delivers full decision intelligence infrastructure — governance, evidence, intelligence — without any external dependency. This capability is unique in the market and commands the highest premium.

**Example 3: Control Value for a Global Bank.** A global bank with complex IT governance, strict change management, and regulatory-mandated update cycles requires self-hosted deployment. The bank values control over update timing, infrastructure integration, and operational autonomy. Self-hosted deployment provides this control without sacrificing any AQLIYA capability. The premium reflects the control value.

## 17. Enterprise Impact

1. **Access to regulated markets.** Self-hosted and air-gapped deployments open regulated markets that cloud-only SaaS cannot serve. These markets are the most valuable and most defensible.
2. **Higher average contract values.** Self-hosted and air-gapped contracts command premium pricing and include professional services revenue.
3. **Stronger lock-in.** Self-hosted deployments with custom governance configuration, local evidence histories, and integrated workflows create deeper enterprise lock-in than cloud subscriptions.
4. **Category reinforcement.** Selling self-hosted decision intelligence infrastructure — not self-hosted audit software — reinforces the Enterprise Decision Intelligence category at every deployment.
5. **Regulatory credibility.** Air-gapped capability demonstrates that AQLIYA's governance and evidence architecture meets the highest regulatory requirements. This credibility extends to all deployment models.

## 18. Long-Term Strategic Importance

Self-hosted value theory is strategically important because AQLIYA's most valuable, most regulated, most defensible customers require it. Government audit offices, defense financial units, central banks, and supranational regulatory bodies cannot use cloud-dependent tools. AQLIYA that cannot serve these buyers is AQLIYA that has left its highest-value market unserved.

The commercial model for self-hosted deployment must reinforce — not undermine — the category thesis. AQLIYA sells decision intelligence infrastructure, and self-hosted delivery is a premium deployment of that infrastructure. Treating self-hosted as a discount tier confuses the category and underprices the product.

Over time, as data sovereignty regulations proliferate globally, the demand for self-hosted decision intelligence will grow. AQLIYA that has established self-hosted as a first-class, premium deployment model will be positioned to capture this demand. AQLIYA that has treated self-hosted as an afterthought will be rebuilding from scratch.
