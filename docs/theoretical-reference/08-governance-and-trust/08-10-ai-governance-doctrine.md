# AI Governance Doctrine

## 1. Purpose

This document defines how AI capabilities are bounded, governed, inspected, and positioned inside AQLIYA.

## 2. Thesis

**AI in AQLIYA is governed assistance embedded inside evidence-backed workflows; it is never an autonomous authority, product identity, or substitute for accountable human decision-making.**

## 3. Problem

AI creates pressure toward speed, novelty, and autonomy. In regulated domains, that pressure often produces black-box recommendations, unreviewed automation, and category drift toward chatbot or generic AI SaaS behavior.

## 4. Why Existing Systems Fail

- AI is layered on top of weak workflow controls
- model outputs lack explanation and evidence trace
- products optimize prompt interaction instead of governed work
- vendors confuse AI capability with trustworthiness

## 5. AQLIYA Philosophy

AQLIYA is Enterprise Decision Intelligence infrastructure, not an AI product seeking a use case. AuditOS is the first wedge, and Financial Intelligence is the first moat. Evidence is the unit of trust, and AI serves those doctrines by assisting reviewers within governed workflows. AI assists. Humans decide. Evidence governs.

## 6. Core Principles

1. AI is embedded, not sovereign.
2. AI outputs must be evidence-backed and explainable.
3. AI authority is bounded by workflow governance.
4. Output trust depends on data and evidence quality.
5. Human accountability is never displaced.
6. AI posture must not create chatbot, dashboard, or automation-agency drift.

## 7. Key Concepts

- **AI-Assisted Output:** A signal, recommendation, extraction, or ranking produced with model assistance.
- **Trusted AI Path:** The governed path where model outputs may influence material decisions after review.
- **Low-Trust AI Path:** Drafting or convenience use that does not directly support material governed decisions.
- **AI Control Boundary:** The explicit limit of what AI may do autonomously, if anything.

## 8. Operational Implications

1. Teams must classify AI use cases by risk and allowed authority.
2. High-impact AI functions require stronger review and release discipline.
3. Feedback loops should capture acceptance, rejection, and modification outcomes.
4. Customer communication must position AI as assistive infrastructure, not autonomous magic.

## 9. Product Implications

AI functionality should appear where work happens: evidence review, signal triage, finding drafting, and anomaly analysis. It should not become a free-form chat surface that bypasses workflow state and governance context.

## 10. Architecture Implications

1. Separation between trusted and low-trust AI flows.
2. Retention of model version, prompt or input context, and explanation artifacts where appropriate.
3. Access-controlled retrieval and inference boundaries.
4. Feature flags and policy enforcement for high-risk AI capabilities.

## 11. Governance Implications

Governance is structural, not procedural. Governance should define which AI outputs may influence which workflow stages, what evidence and explanation are required, and when human approval is mandatory. It should also prohibit unreviewed autonomous closure of regulated review tasks.

## 12. AI / Intelligence Implications

AI may suggest, extract, summarize, rank, and draft. AI may not autonomously accept evidence, approve findings, finalize report conclusions, or make liability-bearing professional decisions. High-confidence model output does not override governance rules.

## 13. UX Implications

The UX should make AI contribution visible but bounded. Users should know what the model did, what evidence supports it, what remains uncertain, and what human action is still required.

## 14. Commercial Implications

AI governance helps AQLIYA avoid the commodity trap of generic AI vendors. It supports enterprise trust and premium positioning by showing that the company solves governed decision work, not just text generation.

## 15. Anti-Patterns

1. **Copilot Identity Drift.** Reframing AQLIYA as a generic AI assistant.
2. **Autonomy Creep.** Gradually letting AI close or approve material work.
3. **Prompt-As-Workflow.** Letting chat replace governed review structure.
4. **Model Confidence Worship.** Treating confidence scores as substitutes for evidence and authority.
5. **Automation Agency Drift.** Turning the platform into bespoke AI automation services.

## 16. Examples

**Example 1:** AI drafts a finding summary from accepted evidence, but the reviewer edits and approves the final wording.

**Example 2:** AI suggests a cluster of related anomalies, yet the manager decides which items become formal findings.

**Example 3:** A model proposes evidence links, but acceptance remains blocked until a reviewer verifies provenance and relevance.

## 17. Enterprise Impact

1. Safer AI adoption in regulated environments.
2. Lower professional and compliance risk.
3. Better reviewer trust in AI assistance.
4. Stronger differentiation from generic AI products.

## 18. Long-Term Strategic Importance

AI governance protects AQLIYA's doctrine as it scales. It ensures AI deepens the company's moat instead of redefining the company as a chatbot, dashboard, or automation vendor.
