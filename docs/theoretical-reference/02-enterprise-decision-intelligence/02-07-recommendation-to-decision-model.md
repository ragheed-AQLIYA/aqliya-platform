# Recommendation-To-Decision Model

## 1. Purpose

This document defines the transition model by which recommendations become decisions within the Enterprise Decision Intelligence lifecycle. It specifies the structural relationship between recommendations and decisions, the review process that governs the transition, the evidence requirements that must be satisfied, and the governance rules that constrain how the transition occurs.

The recommendation-to-decision transition is the most critical point in the decision lifecycle. It is where intelligence becomes action, where suggestion becomes commitment, and where the system's design philosophy — AI assists, humans decide — is structurally enforced.

## 2. Thesis

**A recommendation becomes a decision only through a governed human review step in which the reviewer examines evidence, evaluates the recommendation, and explicitly accepts, rejects, or modifies it within the decision lifecycle.**

The transition is never automatic. In regulated, liability-bearing domains — audit, financial intelligence, governance — autonomous execution of AI-generated recommendations is structurally prohibited. The human review step is not a product choice. It is a design principle derived from the category's philosophical commitment that AI augments judgment, not replaces it.

The recommendation and the decision are separate, linked objects. A decision that does not reference its originating recommendation has an incomplete trace. A recommendation that is not linked to a subsequent decision is an unresolved signal.

## 3. Problem

Recommendations and decisions are conflated throughout enterprise software:

- **AI copilots conflate them.** A user asks a chatbot a question. The chatbot generates a recommendation. If the user acts on it, there is no record that a recommendation was made, that it was evaluated, or that it was accepted. The recommendation and decision are collapsed into a single, untraceable interaction.
- **Dashboards conflate them.** A dashboard shows a metric and suggests an action. The user decides to take that action. There is no structured link between the dashboard insight and the resulting decision.
- **Recommendations are implicit.** A report recommends a course of action. A meeting endorses it. Someone executes it. There is no system — only a chain of human communication that produces no structured trace.
- **Accountability is fractured.** When a recommendation leads to a bad outcome, there is no authoritative record of who made the recommendation, what evidence it was based on, who accepted it, and what reasoning supported the acceptance.

The result: organizations cannot answer the most fundamental question about their AI-assisted decision processes — "did we follow the recommendation, and why or why not?"

## 4. Why Existing Systems Fail

| Category | What It Does | Recommendation-Decision Gap |
|---|---|---|
| **AI Copilots / Chatbots** | Generates text responses to user queries | No structured recommendation object. No link between response and subsequent action. Stateless by design. |
| **BI Platforms** | Generates charts and dashboards with insights | Insights are implicit recommendations. No system captures whether the insight led to a decision or what the decision was. |
| **Recommendation Engines** | Suggests products, content, or actions | Designed for low-stakes, high-volume contexts. No governance, no evidence trace, no structural review step. |
| **Workflow Systems** | Routes tasks through approval steps | Approves tasks, not recommendations. Does not distinguish between approving an action and evaluating a recommendation. |
| **Decision Support Systems** | Provides data and analysis for human decision-makers | Supports decision-making but does not structure the recommendation-decision transition. No persistent recommendation object. |
| **Email / Collaboration Tools** | Facilitates human communication about recommendations | Recommendations live in email threads. Decisions live in reply emails. No structured link, no trace, no governance. |

**The common failure pattern:** existing systems either (a) generate recommendations without a mechanism for structured review and decision, or (b) support decision-making without a formal recommendation stage. Neither approach produces a traceable recommendation-to-decision chain.

## 5. AQLIYA Philosophy

**Recommendations and decisions are distinct, linked objects.** A recommendation is a proposed course of action with evidence trace, generated by the intelligence layer or a human. A decision is the reviewer's explicit response to a recommendation: accept, reject, or modify. The link between them is part of the decision trace.

**The human review step is structural, not optional.** The system enforces the review step at the architecture level. There is no path from recommendation to action that bypasses human review in regulated domains.

**Review is not approval.** Review is the act of examining evidence and evaluating a recommendation. Approval is one possible outcome of review. The system records the review process separately from the review outcome.

**Recommendations are traceable to evidence.** Every recommendation carries references to the specific evidence that supports it. The reviewer inspects the evidence through the recommendation, not separate from it.

**The decision records the delta.** When a reviewer modifies a recommendation before accepting it, the system records what changed — the difference between the recommendation and the final decision. This delta is the most information-rich signal in the learning loop.

## 6. Core Principles

1. **Recommendations are first-class objects.** A recommendation has its own schema, lifecycle, evidence references, confidence expression, governance metadata, and trace. It is not a text field or a chat message.

2. **Decisions reference recommendations.** Every decision that originates from a recommendation stores the recommendation ID as a required field. A decision without a recommendation reference has an incomplete trace.

3. **The review step is mandatory and recorded.** The system records the reviewer's identity, the time spent reviewing, the evidence inspected, the recommendation evaluated, and the decision outcome. If no review occurred, the lifecycle is incomplete.

4. **Review outcomes are typed.** The reviewer explicitly selects: Accept (recommendation adopted as stated), Reject (recommendation declined with reasoning), or Modify (recommendation adopted with changes, recorded as a delta).

5. **Recommendations have a lifespan.** A recommendation that is not reviewed within its governance-defined period expires. Expired recommendations are recorded in the trace as unresolved.

6. **Evidence is presented with the recommendation.** The reviewer must see the evidence that supports the recommendation within the same view. Separating evidence from recommendation undermines informed review.

## 7. Key Concepts

- **Recommendation Object:** A structured representation of a proposed decision containing: context, evidence references, proposed action or conclusion, confidence expression, generator (AI model or human), timestamp, and lifecycle status.

- **Decision Record:** The structured record of a reviewer's response to a recommendation: outcome type (accept/reject/modify), reviewer identity, reasoning, evidence inspected, timestamp, and governance metadata.

- **Review Delta:** The difference between a recommendation and the final decision when the reviewer chooses to modify it. The delta is recorded as a structured diff.

- **Review Scope:** The set of evidence and recommendation components that the reviewer actually inspected during the review step. The system records which evidence was opened, how much time was spent, and whether all required evidence was reviewed.

- **Recommendation Lifespan:** The governance-defined maximum time between recommendation creation and required review. Lifespans vary by decision type, risk level, and domain.

- **Unresolved Recommendation:** A recommendation that reached the end of its lifespan without a decision. The lifecycle records it as unresolved, which may trigger escalation or automatic rejection by governance rules.

- **Recommendation-to-Decision Chain:** The linked sequence: source data → evidence → signal → recommendation → review → decision. The chain is the atomic trace unit for the recommendation-decision transition.

## 8. Operational Implications

1. Every deployment configures recommendation lifespans per decision type. A materiality finding may have a 48-hour lifespan. A routine classification may have 7 days.
2. Reviewers are trained on the three decision outcomes: accept, reject, modify. The modify outcome includes recording the delta, which requires additional training.
3. Escalation workflows are configured for unresolved recommendations. When a recommendation expires, the system notifies the reviewer's manager and reassigns or auto-escalates.
4. Evidence presentation standards are defined per domain. An audit finding recommendation must present the specific document and entry that triggered the finding.
5. Quality assurance teams monitor review patterns — review time, evidence inspection rate, delta frequency — to identify review quality issues.
6. Unresolved recommendation rates are tracked as a governance metric. High unresolved rates indicate process bottlenecks or inadequate review capacity.

## 9. Product Implications

1. The recommendation view is separate from the decision view but linked. A user viewing a decision can navigate to the recommendation that originated it and the evidence that supported it.
2. The review interface presents the recommendation and its evidence in a unified layout — recommendation on one side, key evidence on the other.
3. Decision outcomes are explicit buttons or actions: Accept, Reject (with reason field), Modify (with delta editing interface).
4. The recommendation list is a product surface. Users see all recommendations assigned to them, with status (pending review, accepted, rejected, modified, expired).
5. Unresolved recommendations are visually distinct. Expired recommendations trigger a governance notification flow.
6. Review analytics are available per reviewer, per team, and per recommendation type — showing review time, evidence inspection rate, and outcome distribution.
7. The recommendation object supports comments and collaboration without changing the formal recommendation-decision structure. Reviewers can annotate evidence and ask clarifying questions within the recommendation lifecycle.

## 10. Architecture Implications

1. The recommendation object and the decision record are separate but linked data types within the decision engine. The link is a first-class reference enforced at the schema level.
2. The review lifecycle is a sub-lifecycle within the overall decision lifecycle. It has its own stages: pending review, under review, decision recorded.
3. Evidence inspection tracking is an event stream. Each time a reviewer opens an evidence document or data point, the event is recorded in the decision trace.
4. The review delta is stored as a structured diff object — a machine-readable representation of what changed between recommendation and decision.
5. Recommendation lifespan enforcement is a governance rule executed by the decision engine. When a recommendation reaches its lifespan end without a decision, the engine triggers the configured escalation workflow.
6. The recommendation-to-decision chain is queryable at scale. The system supports "find all recommendations of type X that were rejected, and show the associated evidence and reviewer reasoning."
7. The architecture supports multiple recommendation sources — AI models, human experts, external systems — with consistent recommendation object schema regardless of source.

## 11. Governance Implications

1. Governance rules specify who can generate recommendations (by role, by AI model, by domain), who can review recommendations (by role, by certification, by seniority), and who can make final decisions.
2. The review step is governed by minimum evidence inspection requirements. A reviewer may be required to inspect specified evidence before the decision can be recorded.
3. Governance rules define recommendation lifespans per decision type. High-risk decisions may have shorter lifespans to prevent decision paralysis.
4. Multi-reviewer governance is supported. Some decision types may require multiple independent reviews before a recommendation can proceed to decision.
5. Approval authority is recorded in the governance layer. A junior reviewer may accept a recommendation, but rejection or modification of a high-materiality recommendation may require senior approval.
6. The recommendation-to-decision governance model supports regulatory requirements for separation of duties — the person who generates the recommendation and the person who decides must be different actors.
7. All governance interactions — rule evaluations, approval authority checks, separation-of-duty verifications — are recorded in the governance trace linked to the recommendation-to-decision chain.

## 12. AI / Intelligence Implications

1. AI-generated recommendations are clearly labeled with the model identifier, version, and confidence expression. The reviewer knows whether a recommendation came from an ML model, a rules-based system, or a human.
2. The intelligence layer uses review outcomes as training feedback. Accepted recommendations reinforce the model. Rejected and modified recommendations are analyzed for pattern adjustment.
3. Review delta analysis is an intelligence function. The system analyzes patterns in how reviewers modify AI recommendations — which parts are consistently changed, what additional evidence reviewers frequently add — and adjusts recommendation generation accordingly.
4. AI recommendations include confidence expressions in domain-relevant terms, not just probabilistic scores. An audit recommendation expresses confidence as evidence strength and materiality level.
5. The intelligence layer can generate counter-recommendations when evidence changes. If new evidence contradicts an active recommendation, the system generates an updated recommendation and flags the original as superseded.
6. AI models never auto-execute. The recommendation-to-decision review step is structurally enforced at the architecture level. No configuration exists to bypass it.

## 13. UX Implications

1. The review interface is designed for high-throughput decision-making. Keyboard shortcuts for accept, reject, and modify. Batch review of multiple recommendations of the same type.
2. Evidence is presented in a context panel alongside the recommendation. The reviewer does not navigate away from the recommendation to examine evidence.
3. The modify interface shows a side-by-side diff editor — recommendation on the left, editable decision on the right, with changes highlighted.
4. Recommendation lists are filterable by status, type, domain, generator, lifespan remaining, and priority.
5. Visual indicators show recommendation urgency — color-coded by lifespan remaining, with escalation warnings.
6. Review history is accessible from the recommendation view. Reviewers see whether a recommendation has been previously reviewed and modified by another reviewer.
7. Mobile review supports accept/reject with brief reasoning but recommends full desktop review for complex recommendations requiring evidence inspection.

## 14. Commercial Implications

1. The recommendation-to-decision model is a core differentiator against AI copilots and chatbots. The commercial message: "they generate suggestions. We structure the chain from suggestion to decision to outcome."
2. Regulated customers — audit firms, financial institutions, government agencies — pay a premium for the structural enforcement of human review. The model reduces regulatory risk.
3. Proof-of-concept demonstrations include a comparison: show an AI-generated recommendation in a chatbot (untraceable) vs. an AQLIYA recommendation (structured, evidence-linked, reviewable, traceable).
4. Recommendation review analytics are a retention driver. Organizations that see review quality metrics improve are unlikely to return to unstructured recommendation-consumption workflows.
5. The model supports expansion pricing: more decision types, more recommendation sources, more complex governance configurations.
6. The unresolved recommendation rate is a customer health metric. High rates indicate process issues that, if unaddressed, may lead to churn.

## 15. Anti-Patterns

1. **Auto-Execution.** Allowing AI-generated recommendations to execute without human review. In regulated domains, this is professional liability. In any domain, it violates the principle that AI assists, humans decide.

2. **Recommendation as Chat Message.** Treating recommendations as chat messages — ephemeral, unstructured, untraceable. A recommendation is a structured object with lifecycle and evidence requirements.

3. **Decision Without Recommendation Reference.** Recording a decision without linking it to the recommendation that originated it. This breaks the recommendation-to-decision chain and produces an incomplete trace.

4. **Review as Rubber Stamp.** Designing a review step that reviewers can bypass or execute without meaningful evidence inspection. If the system does not record evidence inspection, the review step is theater.

5. **Single Review Path.** Forcing every recommendation through the same review process regardless of risk level. Low-risk recommendations should have streamlined review. High-risk recommendations require full evidence inspection and multi-party approval.

6. **Recommendation Without Evidence.** Generating recommendations without linking to specific evidence. A recommendation without evidence is an opinion, not a decision support artifact.

7. **Silent Expiration.** Allowing recommendations to expire without notification, escalation, or trace recording. Expired recommendations must be recorded and escalated according to governance policy.

8. **Modify Without Delta.** Allowing reviewers to modify recommendations without recording what changed. The delta is the most valuable learning signal in the recommendation-to-decision transition. Losing it undermines the learning loop.

## 16. Examples

**Example 1: Audit Finding Review.** AuditOS generates a finding: "Journal entry 1047 exceeds materiality threshold and lacks supporting documentation." The recommendation object includes: the specific entry, the materiality calculation, the client's supporting document (flagged as missing), and the confidence expression (evidence strength: medium, materiality: high). The engagement reviewer opens the recommendation, inspects the evidence, reviews the client's explanation (attached as a follow-up document), and decides: Modify — accept the finding but reclassify it from "material" to "immaterial" based on the client's explanation. The system records the delta: materiality classification changed from "material" to "immaterial" with the reviewer's reasoning. The trace links the original recommendation, the modified decision, and the evidence that supported the modification.

**Example 2: Multi-Reviewer Governance.** A financial intelligence module generates a high-risk recommendation: "Flag transaction 8821 for suspicious activity reporting." Governance rules require two independent reviews before submission. Reviewer A inspects the evidence, agrees, and accepts. Reviewer B inspects the same evidence, disagrees, and rejects with reasoning: "the transaction pattern matches a known legitimate business arrangement." The system records both reviews, both decisions, and the split outcome. The system escalates to a senior reviewer who makes the final decision. The trace records all three reviews, their evidence inspections, their decisions, and the final outcome. The recommendation-to-decision chain preserves the full governance process.

**Example 3: Expired Recommendation Escalation.** A recommendation for a materiality adjustment on a publicly traded client engagement requires review within 48 hours. The assigned reviewer is on leave. The system sends notifications at 24 hours, 36 hours, and 44 hours. At 48 hours, the recommendation expires. The system escalates to the reviewer's manager, who reviews the now-expired recommendation and makes the decision. The trace records the recommendation lifespan, the escalation events, and the manager's decision, with the original unresolved status flagged for governance review.

## 17. Enterprise Impact

1. **Recommendation accountability.** Every AI-generated recommendation has a traceable outcome — accepted, rejected, modified, or unresolved. Organizations know what their AI is recommending and what happens to those recommendations.
2. **Review quality visibility.** Organizations can measure review quality — evidence inspection rates, review time distributions, delta patterns — and identify reviewers who need additional training or support.
3. **Intelligence improvement.** Modification delta analysis reveals systematic gaps in AI recommendations — areas where human reviewers consistently disagree or supplement.
4. **Governance confidence.** Regulators and clients can see the complete recommendation-to-decision chain for any decision — who recommended, who reviewed, what evidence was considered, what decision was made, and why.
5. **Risk reduction.** The structural enforcement of human review prevents autonomous execution of AI recommendations in domains where that would create liability.
6. **Process optimization.** Unresolved recommendation rates and review cycle times reveal bottlenecks in the decision process, enabling targeted improvement.

## 18. Long-Term Strategic Importance

The recommendation-to-decision model is the structural expression of AQLIYA's core philosophical commitment: AI assists, humans decide. As AI recommendation capabilities grow more sophisticated, the importance of a governed, traceable recommendation-to-decision transition grows with it. The model ensures that as AI becomes more capable, it does not become less accountable.

In a future where AI generates the majority of enterprise recommendations, the organizations that have structured, governed, traceable recommendation-to-decision processes will be the organizations that can safely deploy advanced AI in regulated domains. Organizations without this model will face a choice: either restrict AI to low-risk recommendations or deploy AI recommendations without accountability.

AQLIYA's long-term position depends on the recommendation-to-decision model becoming the standard architecture for AI-assisted decision-making in regulated enterprise domains — the way segregation of duties is a standard control in financial systems.
